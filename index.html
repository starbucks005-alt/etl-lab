THREE WORKSHOP TRACKS
TRACK 1
Human-Led, Minimal AI

The original classroom method. Researchers learn the full SLR process — keyword architecture, Boolean search logic, subgroup construction, gap identification — before any AI acceleration. Excel-based with PubMed and OpenAlex. Human judgment at every decision point. This is where scientific rigor is built. The zeros are not failures. They are the point.

TRACK 2
AI-Assisted

Human-led with SLR Studio augmenting the process. AI generates keyword pools, identifies foundation papers across 25 subgroups, surfaces literature gaps, and scaffolds analysis. The researcher makes every interpretive decision. Includes Gap Mining, Thesis Development, and Poster modes. The researcher drives. The studio navigates.

TRACK 3
Full SLR Studio Pipeline

End-to-end SLR Studio workflow. Keyword architecture → live OpenAlex search → foundation paper identification → gap analysis → PRISMA 2020 compliance → manuscript draft → poster → grant significance sections. Includes all ten Research Tools: Methods Coach, Reviewer Response, Figure Studio, Journal Finder, and more. From first search to submission-ready draft in one session.

For Faculty

SLR Studio Is AI‑Assisted.
Not AI‑Authored.

Students still think, decide, interpret, and write. The system provides structure, evidence, and learning scaffolds. This is a teaching tool, not a writing shortcut.

1. Students Must Do the Intellectual Work

SLR Studio requires students to build a defensible keyword architecture, conduct a systematic search, examine subgroup patterns, identify foundation papers, interpret gaps in the literature, make decisions about relevance and inclusion, and understand how the literature fits together.

These are the core cognitive tasks faculty expect in graduate-level research. The tool does not bypass them. It forces them.

2. All Writing Is Grounded in the Student's Actual Sources

Generic LLMs often hallucinate citations or invent findings. SLR Studio avoids this because students upload their own PDFs, the system extracts real metadata, the SLR table and author table are built from actual retrieved papers, and any generated text must cite from the student's evidence set. This ensures traceability, verifiability, and academic integrity.

3. Writing Support Is Section-Based, Not "Write My Paper"

SLR Studio never produces a full literature review from a single prompt. It guides students through individual components: writing the problem statement, drafting the background section using specific papers, summarizing the gap identified in a particular subgroup. This is instructional scaffolding, not ghostwriting.

Students still read, interpret, select, synthesize, revise, and defend. The AI helps them express what they have already learned.

4. The Tool Teaches Research Structure, Not Shortcuts

SLR Studio mirrors the way faculty teach: breaking the literature review into conceptual parts, showing examples of tone and structure, encouraging evidence-based writing, reinforcing the logic of scholarly argumentation. Students learn why each section exists and how it contributes to the whole. This is a pedagogical model, not a productivity hack.

5. The Output Is a Draft, Not a Finished Product

Everything SLR Studio generates is editable, revisable, transparent, traceable, and based on the student's own sources. The student must refine, integrate, and defend the work. This aligns with the expectations of graduate committees and accreditation bodies.

6. Faculty Retain Full Oversight

Because the system is structured and evidence-based, faculty can easily see what papers were used, how the student organized the literature, where the gaps came from, how the argument was constructed, and what the AI generated versus what the student wrote. This transparency is impossible with generic LLMs.

The Bottom Line

SLR Studio is an AI-assisted learning environment, not an AI writing replacement. It strengthens research skills, reduces hallucinations, and keeps students grounded in real evidence. The student remains the thinker, the analyst, and the author. This is the distinction that matters.
